# The MA ADU Parking Rule, Decoded

Parking is the single most-disputed correction on Massachusetts ADU permits after Chapter 150. Cities default to their pre-2025 parking minimums — typically two spaces per dwelling, sometimes more — and write the requirement onto every plan-check letter. The state law caps what they can require at one additional space, and zero within 0.5 miles of qualifying transit. This guide walks through the rule, the exemption, the edge cases, and the response template that resolves preempted parking corrections.

## The state ceiling

Two citations. The first is the statute:

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleVII/Chapter40A/Section3 | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: MGL Ch 40A § 3 — parking ceiling 1 additional space, 0 within 0.5 mi of qualifying transit]

The second is the implementing regulation:

[source: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/accessory-dwelling-units | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: 760 CMR 71.00 — EOHLC implementing regulation, parking section]

Together they say: a city cannot require more than one additional off-street parking space for a Protected Use ADU, and cannot require any additional space if the parcel sits within 0.5 miles of a qualifying transit facility.

This is a **ceiling**, not a floor. The city can require less. The city cannot require more.

## What counts as a qualifying transit facility

This is the most-litigated phrase in the rule. The statute language is "commuter rail, subway, ferry, or bus station." Plain reading:

- **Commuter rail** — any MBTA commuter rail station. The Worcester, Providence, Newburyport, Lowell, Fitchburg, and Franklin lines all qualify.
- **Subway** — every Red, Orange, Blue, Green, and Silver Line station.
- **Ferry** — MBTA ferry terminals (Hingham, Hull, Long Wharf, etc.) and other regulated ferry terminals.
- **Bus station** — and this is where the dispute lives.

"Bus station" is not the same as "bus stop." A bus station is a designated facility — typically a terminal or a major transfer point with shelters, signage, and named identification. A bus stop is a sign on a pole.

Conservative reading: only designated MBTA bus terminals (Quincy Center, Forest Hills, Sullivan Square, Maverick, Ashmont, Dudley/Nubian, Wonderland, etc.) qualify. Aggressive reading: any bus stop with regular service. EOHLC has not issued a definitive interpretive letter as of mid-2026.

**Practical advice for contractors:** measure to the nearest unambiguous transit facility. If you are within 0.5 miles of a subway station, commuter rail station, or named MBTA bus terminal, the parking exemption applies. If your closest qualifying facility is a bus stop, expect the city to push back — and have a backup argument ready (such as the proximity to a nearby subway station several blocks farther but still within range).

## How to measure 0.5 miles

Walking distance, not straight-line. The statute does not specify, but EOHLC's published guidance and standard zoning practice for similar transit-proximity rules (the MBTA Communities Act, for example) use walking distance along public ways. Google Maps walking directions, OSRM, or any standard routing engine is acceptable.

The half-mile measurement starts at the parcel boundary, not the dwelling unit's front door. For an irregular parcel where part is within 0.5 miles and part is not, the closer point governs.

## The owner-occupancy parking subtlety

Some Massachusetts cities historically required additional parking when an ADU was occupied by a non-owner — an effective owner-occupancy condition through the parking lever. Chapter 150 closed that loop. Owner-occupancy itself is preempted. Conditional parking based on owner-occupancy status is preempted as a downstream consequence.

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/SessionLaws/Acts/2024/Chapter150 | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: St. 2024, c. 150 § 8 — amends MGL Ch 40A § 3 to preempt owner-occupancy and dependent conditions]

If a city writes a correction that says "ADU requires two parking spaces unless owner-occupied," that is a preempted condition under both the parking ceiling and the owner-occupancy preemption — argue both.

## What cities can still legitimately regulate

The state law caps the **number** of required spaces. It does not cap:

- **Dimensional requirements for the spaces themselves** — minimum 9 by 18 feet, hardscape vs gravel, drainage, accessibility.
- **Curb-cut and driveway requirements** under separate municipal authority.
- **Off-street vs on-street parking allocation** within the parcel.
- **Tandem or stacked parking** acceptability — most cities allow tandem for one of the required spaces, but the state rule does not require them to.

A city can also require zero additional spaces. The exemption-near-transit is a ceiling, not a mandate.

## The response template when a city requires more than one space

Use this verbatim or close to it. Most plan checkers withdraw the comment after one round.

> "The proposed ADU is a Protected Use under MGL Ch 40A § 3 (as amended by St. 2024, c. 150, § 8) and is implemented by 760 CMR 71.00. The state law caps off-street parking at one additional space for an ADU on a single-family parcel, and at zero additional spaces where the parcel sits within 0.5 miles of a qualifying transit facility.
>
> The parcel at [address] is [within / not within] 0.5 miles walking distance of [qualifying facility — name and type], measured along public ways. The applicable parking requirement is therefore [zero / one] additional space.
>
> The [city]'s requirement of [N] additional spaces in correction item [#] is preempted to the extent it exceeds the state ceiling. We respectfully request the comment be revised to require [zero / one] additional space, consistent with state law."

Attach a map showing the parcel and the nearest qualifying facility with the walking distance measurement labeled. Most reviewers acknowledge the citation and revise the comment immediately.

## When to escalate

If a plan checker insists on more than one space after a clean preemption response, the next move is a written request for the city's legal basis. Specifically: ask which authority within the city's zoning bylaw or general bylaws permits requiring more parking than the state ceiling, and whether that authority has been reviewed against the EOHLC implementing regulation. Cities that cannot answer typically withdraw the comment.

If the response is "we have not updated our bylaw yet" — that is not a defense. State law preempts conflicting local bylaws by operation of law on the effective date (February 2, 2025). The local bylaw does not need to be amended for the preemption to apply.

If escalation does not resolve, the next step is administrative appeal to the local zoning board (typically the Board of Appeals or equivalent) or, if necessary, judicial review. EOHLC has been clear in its FAQ that the parking ceiling is statewide and absolute — most disputes settle before they reach formal appeal.

## What the rule does not change

- **Construction-phase parking** during demolition and construction is not part of the protected-use parking analysis. Cities can require construction parking management plans without conflict.
- **Curb cuts and driveway widening** are governed by separate authority. Adding a new curb cut to accommodate a required parking space may require its own approval.
- **Public way accessibility** under the Americans with Disabilities Act and Massachusetts Architectural Access Board (521 CMR) applies to public-facing aspects of the parking, not to the count.
