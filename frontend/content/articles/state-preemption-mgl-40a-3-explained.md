# State Preemption Under MGL Ch 40A §3 — When Massachusetts Law Beats Your Local Bylaw

If you're building an ADU anywhere in Massachusetts and your city plan checker requires owner-occupancy, special-permit hearings, or extra parking, there's a good chance the requirement is preempted by state law. This guide explains the preemption mechanism, draws the line between USE (state-controlled) and FORM (locally controlled), and shows the citation pattern that wins most preempted-correction disputes.

## What changed in 2024

Massachusetts passed **Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024** in August of that year. The act amended several sections of MGL Chapter 40A — the state zoning enabling act — to make ADUs by-right in single-family zones across the state.

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/SessionLaws/Acts/2024/Chapter150 | retrieved: 2026-05-05 | citation: St. 2024, c. 150 §§ 7-8 amend MGL Ch 40A §§ 1A and 3]

The amendments took effect in their substantive provisions on February 2, 2025. The state then promulgated **760 CMR 71.00**, the implementing regulations, on the same date.

[source: https://www.mass.gov/regulations/760-CMR-7100-affordable-homes-act-adu-regulations | retrieved: 2026-05-05 | citation: 760 CMR 71.00 implementing regulations effective 2025-02-02]

Before this change, ADUs in Massachusetts were governed entirely by local zoning. Each city set its own rules — some allowed ADUs by special permit, some banned them, some allowed only attached units, some required owner-occupancy. Chapter 150 collapsed that variation into a single statewide floor.

## What the floor says

The amended MGL Ch 40A § 3 prohibits municipalities from doing any of the following with respect to a single ADU on a single-family lot:

- **Banning ADUs.** They are now by-right in any single-family zone.
- **Requiring a special permit or public hearing.** By-right means by-right; no discretionary review for the use itself.
- **Requiring owner-occupancy.** This is the most-litigated element of the new law. A municipality cannot condition ADU permit issuance on the owner living in either unit.
- **Requiring more than one parking space.** The state caps parking at one space per ADU.
- **Requiring any parking near MBTA service.** If the parcel is within one-half mile of MBTA service (subway, commuter rail, key bus routes per the MBTA's official map), the city cannot require any parking for the ADU.

The size limit, written into the same section, caps the ADU at the **lesser of 900 square feet or 50% of the primary dwelling's gross floor area**.

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/SessionLaws/Acts/2024/Chapter150 | retrieved: 2026-05-05 | citation: St. 2024, c. 150 §7 — by-right provision, owner-occupancy preemption, parking cap, 900 sqft / 50% size limit]

These rules together are what we call the "state floor." They are the minimum every Massachusetts ADU project gets regardless of the local code.

## What cities can still regulate

Preemption applies to USE — whether you can build an ADU and what the basic terms are. It does **not** preempt FORM — the dimensional rules every building has to follow.

Cities still control:

- **Setbacks.** Front, side, and rear distances from the lot lines. Local dimensional standards apply.
- **Building height.** The state law doesn't override local height limits.
- **Lot coverage and FAR.** Local maximums on how much of the lot you can build on.
- **Site plan review.** A city can require site plan review (different from special-permit review) for things like drainage, fire-apparatus access, and tree preservation.
- **Building code.** 780 CMR is the state code; cities apply it without amendment.
- **Energy code.** Cities choose between Base / Stretch / Specialized at adoption time; once chosen, all new construction in that city must comply.
- **Historic district design review.** State law doesn't preempt independent landmarks commissions.
- **Wetlands setback.** The Wetlands Protection Act has its own preemption layer that runs alongside zoning.

This split — state for USE, locality for FORM — is the fundamental rule. Every ADU correction-letter response works through this distinction.

## The five most-common preempted corrections

Across the Boston ISD corrections letters our skill base covers, five preempted requirements show up repeatedly:

### 1. Owner-occupancy required

**Pattern:** "ADU may only be permitted if the owner occupies one of the dwelling units."

**Response:** Owner-occupancy requirements were prohibited by Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024, §7, which amended MGL Ch 40A §3 to make ADUs by-right without conditions on occupancy. The proposed ADU does not need to be owner-occupied to comply with state law.

### 2. Special permit hearing required

**Pattern:** "ADU requires special permit approval from the Zoning Board."

**Response:** ADUs are by-right in single-family zones under MGL Ch 40A §3 as amended. By-right use is, by definition, not subject to special permit. Cite St. 2024, c. 150, §7.

### 3. Parking for ADU near MBTA stop

**Pattern:** "Provide one off-street parking space for the new ADU."

**Response:** If the parcel is within 0.5 miles of MBTA service: identify the nearest stop by name and the walking distance, cite St. 2024, c. 150 §7, and cite the MBTA station list. Zero parking is required.

### 4. ADU restricted to attached only (or detached only)

**Pattern:** "Detached ADUs are not permitted in this zone."

**Response:** Chapter 150 makes ADUs (attached, detached, or interior) by-right in single-family zones. Cite St. 2024, c. 150 §7. Interior conversion of basement / attic also qualifies.

### 5. ADU restricted to family-member occupancy

**Pattern:** "ADU may only be occupied by family members of the primary residents."

**Response:** Chapter 150 prohibits occupancy restrictions based on family relationship. The ADU may be rented to any tenant. Cite St. 2024, c. 150 §7.

## What's NOT preempted (don't try to dispute these)

Some requirements that LOOK like they should be preempted are not:

- **Side setback of 7 feet.** That's a dimensional requirement, locally controlled.
- **Maximum building height of 30 feet.** Same.
- **Site plan review for drainage and fire apparatus access.** Site plan review is procedural, not a special permit on the use.
- **Stretch Energy Code requirement.** The energy code is locally adopted but it applies to all construction, not specifically to ADUs.
- **780 CMR 10th Edition compliance.** State building code applies regardless.
- **Landmark District design review.** Independent commission, not a zoning special permit.
- **Wetlands buffer setback.** Different statute (Wetlands Protection Act), different preemption layer.

If a correction asks for any of these, the response is to comply with the request, not to argue preemption.

## How to write a preemption response

A clean preemption response has four elements:

1. **Quote the correction.** Verbatim, so there's no ambiguity about which item you're addressing.
2. **State the preemption.** "This requirement is preempted by..." Cite the statute and the implementing regulation if there is one.
3. **Provide the citation chain.** MGL Ch 40A § 3, as amended by St. 2024, c. 150, §7. If applicable, 760 CMR 71.00 § X.
4. **Propose the next step.** "Please confirm that this item is withdrawn so we can finalize the submittal."

The four-step structure prevents the response from reading as adversarial. The plan checker is following local code, not personally trying to block your project. A respectful, well-cited response usually clears the item in the next correction cycle.

## When a plan checker pushes back

Most plan checkers in Massachusetts have caught up to the state law. A small minority will push back on a preemption claim, often by citing the local code as if it were still controlling. The response in that case:

1. Re-cite the state statute and emphasize that under the state preemption framework, conflicting local provisions are unenforceable.
2. If they continue to enforce, escalate to the city's law department or the building commissioner.
3. As a last resort, an Attorney General complaint under the new state law's enforcement mechanism is available.

[source: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/eohlc-affordable-homes-act-adu-resources | retrieved: 2026-05-05 | citation: EOHLC enforcement guidance for ADU state preemption]

In our experience across the corrections letters our skill base covers, escalation past step 1 is rare. Plan checkers respect the state law once it's clearly cited.

## The preemption-watch checklist

When you're reading a Massachusetts ADU corrections letter, scan for any item that touches:

- Owner occupancy
- Special permit / variance / public hearing
- Parking quantity (vs. dimensional parking standards)
- Use type restriction (attached vs. detached vs. interior)
- Family-only occupancy
- Tenancy restrictions
- Special use district restrictions on ADUs

Any of these is a preemption candidate. Cite the state law floor and ask for the item to be withdrawn.

## What this means for your project

Before you respond to a Massachusetts ADU corrections letter, sort the items into three buckets:

1. **State-preempted (USE).** Cite Ch 150 / 760 CMR 71.00 and ask for withdrawal.
2. **Locally controlled (FORM).** Comply or revise the design.
3. **Independent regime (Landmarks, Wetlands, Energy Code).** Different statute; comply.

Most contractor headaches come from missing the first bucket — accepting a correction that the city had no authority to issue.
