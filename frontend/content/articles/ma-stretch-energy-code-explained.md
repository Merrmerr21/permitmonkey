# Massachusetts Stretch Energy Code 225 CMR 22 — A Contractor's Guide for ADU Projects

If you're building an Accessory Dwelling Unit anywhere in Massachusetts, the energy code your project lands on depends on which of three tiers your municipality has adopted. The wrong assumption here is one of the most common reasons a Boston ADU correction letter shows up.

This guide explains the three-tier structure, which cities have adopted what, and the specific compliance pathways available under the Specialized Opt-In Code that Boston, Cambridge, Somerville, Newton, and Brookline all run.

## The three-tier structure

Massachusetts gives every municipality a choice between three energy code tiers, all under 225 CMR 22:

**Base Code** — IECC 2021 with Massachusetts amendments. The state default. Roughly 30% of MA municipalities by adoption count, mostly smaller and more rural towns.

**Stretch Energy Code** — Higher performance than Base. Adopted by the majority of MA municipalities. The most common tier you'll encounter outside of dense urban areas.

**Specialized Opt-In Code** — 225 CMR 22 Appendix RC. The most aggressive tier; net-zero or near-net-zero performance. About 60 municipalities including Boston, Cambridge, Somerville, Newton, and Brookline.

[source: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/stretch-energy-code-development-2023 | retrieved: 2026-05-05 | citation: 225 CMR 22 — three-tier MA energy code structure]

The tier your city has adopted is binding on every new ADU you build there. There's no opt-out for individual projects.

## Boston is on Specialized

For ADUs in Boston, the city has adopted the Specialized Opt-In Code. This is a critical fact that drives most of the design decisions on a Boston ADU project.

[source: https://www.boston.gov/departments/inspectional-services | retrieved: 2026-05-05 | citation: Boston has adopted the Specialized Opt-In Code (225 CMR 22 Appendix RC)]

Cambridge, Somerville, Newton, and Brookline are also on Specialized. If you're working in any of those municipalities, the same compliance pathways apply.

## Three compliance pathways under Specialized

The Specialized Opt-In Code gives you three ways to meet the requirements for an ADU:

### Pathway 1: All-electric

If the ADU has zero on-site fossil fuel combustion (no gas range, no gas water heater, no gas furnace), it qualifies for a more lenient envelope spec. This is the simplest pathway for new ADUs because you're starting from a clean slate.

Heat pump space conditioning, heat pump water heater, induction range. No gas hookup needed; in fact, electrifying the existing primary dwelling at the same time is a common upsell because it simplifies the energy calc.

### Pathway 2: Mixed-fuel + solar PV offset

If you do want gas appliances in the ADU, you can still comply with the Specialized Code by adding solar PV sized to offset the additional energy demand from the gas systems. The solar can be on the primary dwelling roof or the ADU roof.

This pathway is increasingly rare for ADU projects because the heat pump water heaters and induction ranges have closed the cost gap with gas equivalents while avoiding the solar requirement.

### Pathway 3: Passive House

The Specialized Code accepts Passive House certification (PHIUS or PHI) as compliance. This pathway is rare for ADUs — the certification overhead is heavy for an 800-1,200 sqft unit — but it's available, and it's the strongest position to be in if the city challenges any aspect of your envelope.

[source: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/stretch-energy-code-development-2023 | retrieved: 2026-05-05 | citation: 225 CMR 22 Appendix RC — three compliance pathways]

## Climate Zone 5 envelope targets

Massachusetts is in IECC Climate Zone 5. Under the Specialized Opt-In Code, the envelope targets that show up on a typical ADU plan check are:

- Wall: R-19 minimum continuous, with R-13 cavity insulation common in 2x4 framing
- Roof / ceiling: R-49 minimum
- Below-grade walls: R-15 continuous on the exterior face, or R-19 on the interior
- Slab edge: R-10 minimum, four feet down
- Windows: U-factor 0.27, SHGC 0.40 (south-facing) / 0.25 (other orientations)

These are minimums; the all-electric pathway typically clears them with margin. The mixed-fuel pathway has to meet them strictly.

## HERS rating expectations

For Specialized compliance, the HERS rating typically lands in the 45-55 range for an all-electric ADU. The lower the better — a HERS index of zero is "net zero energy." Most contractors target 50 to leave headroom for plan-check questions.

A HERS rater is required for the energy compliance documentation. Budget $500-$1,200 for the rating depending on project complexity and the rater's market position.

## HRV/ERV requirements

Mechanical ventilation is required at all envelope tightness levels under the Specialized Code. For an ADU, this typically means an HRV (heat recovery ventilator) or ERV (energy recovery ventilator), sized per ASHRAE 62.2.

Don't try to meet ventilation requirements with bath and kitchen exhaust fans alone — that approach passed under older codes but won't fly under Specialized.

## The seven most common Boston ADU energy corrections

In our review of Boston ISD corrections letters on ADU projects from 2024-2026:

1. **R-value below Specialized Opt-In threshold.** Designer used Stretch values; ISD requires Specialized.
2. **Wrong climate zone.** Designer used Zone 4 values from a previous out-of-state project.
3. **Mixed-fuel ADU without PV offset.** Project specifies a gas range, no solar. Either remove the gas or add the PV.
4. **Missing HRV/ERV.** Bathroom fan plus kitchen exhaust isn't enough; balanced mechanical ventilation is required.
5. **HERS rating not provided.** Compliance documentation must include the rated HERS index from a certified rater.
6. **Window U-factor too high.** Plans show common stock windows at 0.30; Specialized requires 0.27.
7. **Slab edge insulation missing or wrong.** R-10, four feet down, on every exterior face. Easy to miss in mechanical drawings.

## What this means for your project

If you're scoping an ADU in any of the five major MA cities (Boston, Cambridge, Somerville, Newton, Brookline), default to the all-electric Specialized pathway unless you have a specific reason to deviate. It's the cheapest to design, the cleanest to plan-check, and it positions the project well for any future code tightening.

If you're in a Stretch-Code municipality (the majority of MA cities and towns), the same pathways exist but the envelope and HERS targets are less aggressive. The design judgment is the same: all-electric is cheaper to permit.

If you're in a Base Code municipality, you're on the IECC 2021 floor with MA amendments. Most of this article doesn't apply at the Specialized level, but the all-electric design choice still saves on the energy modeling.
