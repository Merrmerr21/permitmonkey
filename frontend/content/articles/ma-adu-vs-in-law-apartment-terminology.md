# ADU vs In-Law Apartment vs Accessory Unit vs ECHO — Massachusetts Terminology

The same housing concept gets four different names in Massachusetts depending on which bylaw, which decade, and which town you are reading. This matters more than it sounds. The legal protections under Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024 attach to "accessory dwelling units" specifically — and several legacy terms in older municipal bylaws now sit in partial-preemption status. This guide walks through what each term means, when they overlap, and which one to use on your permit application.

## The terms in plain language

**Accessory Dwelling Unit (ADU).** The current statewide statutory term. Defined in MGL Ch 40A § 1A as a self-contained dwelling unit on a lot with a primary single-family or two-family dwelling, with separate sleeping, cooking, and sanitary facilities. This is the term the state law protects.

**In-law apartment.** A legacy term referring to a smaller dwelling unit attached to or within a primary residence, traditionally restricted to family members of the property owner. Many older Massachusetts bylaws still use this language. The "family-only" restriction is now preempted; the broader concept overlaps with ADU.

**Accessory apartment.** A bylaw term used in some municipalities to mean approximately the same thing as an ADU. Often synonymous with ADU but with bylaw-specific dimensional or use restrictions that may pre-date Chapter 150.

**ECHO unit (Elder Cottage Housing Opportunity).** A legacy term, primarily 1980s-2000s, referring to a temporary or modular small dwelling intended for elderly family members, typically on the same parcel as the primary residence. Specific to a narrower use case than the modern ADU framework.

**Carriage house, garage apartment, basement apartment.** Descriptive terms for specific physical configurations of an ADU. All three fit within the modern ADU framework if they meet the statutory definition.

**Detached ADU, attached ADU, conversion ADU.** Modern subdivisions within the ADU category based on physical configuration. PermitMonkey uses these distinctions in its eligibility checker because they have different code implications under 780 CMR (especially fire separation per R302).

## What MGL Ch 40A § 1A actually says

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleVII/Chapter40A/Section1A | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: MGL Ch 40A § 1A — definition of accessory dwelling unit]

The state statute defines an ADU as a self-contained dwelling unit with sleeping, cooking, and sanitary facilities. The definition does **not** include:

- A requirement that the occupant be related to the owner of the parcel
- A requirement that either dwelling be owner-occupied
- Restrictions on who can rent the unit
- A maximum age or use limitation tied to the occupant

The definition is functional, not familial. This is the change that closed the legacy "in-law apartment" loop.

## What Chapter 150 § 7 changed in the definition

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/SessionLaws/Acts/2024/Chapter150 | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: St. 2024, c. 150 §§ 7-8 — Affordable Homes Act ADU provisions]

Chapter 150 § 7 amended MGL Ch 40A § 1A to formalize the ADU definition statewide. Before the amendment, the term "accessory dwelling unit" appeared in some statutes but was not centrally defined; many local bylaws had their own definitions, often more restrictive.

After the amendment, the state-level definition operates as a floor. A municipality cannot define the term more restrictively for purposes of by-right protection. A municipality can use a different term in its own bylaw (e.g., "accessory apartment"), but the state-level protection applies to anything that meets the statutory functional definition regardless of the local label.

## The "in-law apartment" preemption issue

Many MA municipal bylaws still contain "in-law apartment" provisions written before 2025. These typically:

- Limit occupancy to family members of the primary-residence owner
- Require owner-occupancy of either the primary or the in-law unit
- Treat the unit as a temporary use that ceases when the family member moves out
- Apply special-permit or conditional-use requirements

Chapter 150 preempts the family-only and owner-occupancy restrictions for any unit that meets the ADU definition. The bylaw's "in-law apartment" terminology survives, but the operative restrictions on family relationship and owner-occupancy do not.

For a contractor reading a city bylaw with "in-law apartment" provisions, the analysis runs:

1. Does the proposed unit meet the MGL Ch 40A § 1A definition of ADU? If yes, the state-floor protections apply.
2. Does the bylaw's "in-law apartment" provision contain dimensional standards (size, height, setback)? Those are FORM and remain in effect.
3. Does the bylaw's "in-law apartment" provision contain family-only or owner-occupancy restrictions? Those are USE and are preempted.
4. Does the bylaw require a special permit for the "in-law apartment"? Preempted for the first ADU on a single-family parcel; may still apply for a second.

## Which term to use on a permit application

For any post-2025 Massachusetts ADU permit, use **"accessory dwelling unit" (ADU)** as the primary term on the cover sheet and application. This invokes the state-floor protections explicitly.

If the local bylaw uses a different term, reference both. Example cover-sheet declaration:

> "Accessory dwelling unit (ADU) per MGL Ch 40A § 1A as amended by St. 2024, c. 150, § 7. Equivalent to 'accessory apartment' under [city] zoning bylaw § [number]. This unit is a Protected Use under MGL Ch 40A § 3 as amended."

This framing makes the state-law claim explicit while showing the bylaw equivalent for the local reviewer. Most plan checkers will follow the state-law lead.

## The naming traps to avoid

**Do not call the unit an "in-law apartment"** on the application unless the bylaw specifically requires that term. The label triggers reviewer associations with pre-2025 family-only restrictions. Use "ADU."

**Do not call the unit a "rental unit"** even if you intend to rent it. The application is for a dwelling unit; rental is a use that follows. Mixing them on the application can confuse the review.

**Do not call it an "addition" if it is a self-contained ADU.** "Addition" suggests added space within the existing dwelling; "ADU" suggests a separate self-contained unit. The fire-separation, energy-code, and zoning analyses differ.

**Do not call it an "ECHO unit"** unless the bylaw specifically uses that term and you intend to invoke its narrow scope. ECHO frameworks were temporary and elderly-restricted; the modern ADU framework is broader and permanent.

## When local bylaws use multiple terms

Some MA bylaws use multiple terms for different categories. Example: a bylaw might define "ADU" as detached new construction, "accessory apartment" as a conversion ADU, and "in-law apartment" as a use restricted to elder family members. In this case:

- All three categories are likely "accessory dwelling units" under the MGL Ch 40A § 1A definition.
- The state-floor protections apply to all three.
- The bylaw's restrictions differ across the three categories — apply the bylaw's specific dimensional rules for the chosen category, but do not accept the bylaw's restrictive family-relationship or owner-occupancy conditions for any category.

## What this guide does not cover

- **National terminology.** California uses "ADU" with similar definitions; other states use "accessory unit" or "secondary suite" or "garage conversion" with their own nuances. The discussion here is Massachusetts-specific.
- **Building-code occupancy classifications.** 780 CMR 10th Edition treats an ADU as a separate dwelling unit for occupancy purposes. The terminology used in 780 CMR is different from the zoning terminology and is a separate analysis.
- **Federal Fair Housing implications.** The shift from family-only to broader-occupancy raises federal Fair Housing Act considerations that some homeowners worry about. Consult an attorney for specifics; the state-floor preemption operates under MA law and does not address federal implications.
