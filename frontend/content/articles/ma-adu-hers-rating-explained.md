# HERS Rating for Massachusetts ADUs

Every Massachusetts ADU permit in a Specialized Opt-In Code municipality requires a HERS rating. Most contractors learn this the hard way — at certificate-of-occupancy time, when a missing HERS rating blocks the final sign-off after the project is otherwise complete. This guide explains what the HERS rating is, what target scores apply, how the rater fits into the design and construction process, and how to budget for it without surprises.

## What HERS is

HERS stands for **Home Energy Rating System**, administered by the Residential Energy Services Network (RESNET). It's a standardized score that predicts a home's annual energy consumption relative to a reference home built to the 2006 IECC at the same size in the same climate.

The scale:
- **HERS 100** = the reference home (2006 IECC construction at the same size and climate).
- **HERS 0** = a net-zero home that produces as much energy as it consumes.
- **HERS 50** = uses half as much energy as the reference home.
- **HERS 130** = uses 30% more energy than the reference home (an older, leaky home).

Lower is better. A new energy-efficient home typically scores HERS 45-65; a passive house can score below 30 or even negative (net-positive).

## When MA ADUs need a HERS rating

Massachusetts has three energy code tiers under 225 CMR 22:

[source: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/specialized-stretch-code | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: 225 CMR 22 — Base, Stretch, and Specialized Opt-In tiers]

1. **Base Code** — IECC 2021 with MA amendments. Most low-density MA municipalities still on this. HERS rating not strictly required for compliance; prescriptive path is acceptable.
2. **Stretch Code** — higher performance. Many MA municipalities have adopted. HERS rating required for performance-path compliance; prescriptive option still available.
3. **Specialized Opt-In Code** — highest tier. Adopted by Boston, Cambridge, Somerville, Newton, Brookline, plus approximately 60 other MA municipalities. **HERS rating required for all new dwelling units, including ADUs.**

For a Specialized Opt-In Code city, plan to engage a HERS rater. The rating is not optional.

## Target HERS scores for MA ADUs in Specialized Opt-In Code

The Specialized Code sets a target HERS Index that varies based on the home's electrification level and renewable energy production. For a typical all-electric ADU without on-site solar:

- **Target HERS Index**: typically 45-55, depending on the parcel's specific climate factors and the dwelling's geometry.
- **Lower with on-site solar**: each kilowatt of installed PV reduces the effective HERS Index. A net-zero project can score under 30.
- **Higher with mixed fuel**: gas heating or hot water increases the index. The Specialized Code disincentivizes fossil fuels — for new construction, the practical compliance path is all-electric.

The actual target for a specific project depends on the rater's energy model. The model accounts for envelope performance, mechanical system efficiency, water heating, ventilation, plug loads, and renewable production.

## How the HERS rater fits into the design process

A HERS rating is not an at-the-end inspection. It's a design-and-build-time engagement:

1. **Pre-design (before drawings finalize)** — engage a certified HERS rater. They review the proposed envelope, mechanical, and water-heating systems and run an initial energy model. This is where design decisions get made: which heat pump, which water heater, which insulation strategy.
2. **Design phase** — the rater iterates with the design team. If the initial model is short of the target HERS Index, the rater identifies the highest-impact changes (better windows, more insulation, smaller capacity equipment).
3. **Permit submittal** — the cover sheet identifies the HERS rater by name and certification, along with the target HERS Index. Some plan checkers also want the preliminary energy model report.
4. **Construction phase** — the rater conducts mid-construction inspections (insulation, air sealing, mechanical commissioning). Failures at this stage require corrections before drywall or before mechanical commissioning is complete.
5. **Final blower-door test and certification** — the rater performs a blower-door test (air leakage) and a duct-leakage test, then certifies the final HERS Index. This certification is what the building department needs for certificate of occupancy.

A typical Specialized Code rating engagement runs $1,500-$3,500 for a single ADU, depending on the rater's involvement at each phase and the complexity of the project.

## What plan reviewers expect to see

For Specialized Opt-In Code municipalities (Boston, Cambridge, Somerville, Newton, Brookline plus others):

- **HERS rater identified by name and certification** on the cover sheet
- **Target HERS Index** for the project
- **Compliance path summary** — performance path with HERS, or any combination
- **Envelope summary table** — wall R-values, ceiling R-values, foundation R-values, window U-factors and SHGC, infiltration rate target
- **Mechanical system summary** — heating equipment make/model and capacity, cooling equipment, water heating method, ventilation method per IRC M1505
- **Renewable energy plan** if applicable — solar PV size, orientation, expected output

A clean Specialized Code submittal answers all of these on the cover sheet or on a dedicated energy-compliance sheet. Missing any of them generates a routine correction.

## Common HERS-related corrections

- **"Identify HERS rater."** Add the name and RESNET certification to the cover sheet.
- **"Provide preliminary HERS report."** Attach the rater's initial energy model.
- **"Confirm target HERS Index complies with Specialized Opt-In Code."** Add the target index and the compliance margin.
- **"Show mechanical equipment supports target HERS."** Specify equipment make/model and confirm rated efficiency aligns with the energy model.
- **"Address insulation continuity at thermal bridges."** A correction often issued after the mid-construction inspection — addresses joist hangers, framing penetrations, foundation-to-wall transitions where insulation is interrupted.

## Budgeting for HERS — the practical line items

For a typical 720 sqft ADU on Specialized Code:

- **HERS rater fee**: $1,500-$3,500 depending on involvement
- **Heat pump space conditioning** (vs gas furnace): $3,000-$8,000 premium for new construction
- **Heat-pump water heater** (vs gas): $1,500-$3,000 premium
- **Higher-performance windows** (U-0.30 or better): $1,000-$3,000 premium for the ADU
- **Continuous insulation on walls** (vs cavity-only): $2,000-$5,000
- **Air-sealing and blower-door target compliance** (3 ACH50 or better): $500-$2,000 in materials and labor

Rough total premium for Specialized Code compliance over base: $10,000-$25,000 on a typical ADU. This is the cost of the energy code, not the cost of the ADU. Plan for it in the project budget; it is not a contingency item.

## How to read a HERS rating in a plan checker's response

Plan checkers occasionally cite specific HERS values in corrections. The structure to look for:

- **Target HERS Index for the project** (set by the Specialized Code's worksheet)
- **Modeled HERS Index** (what the rater's energy model predicts)
- **As-built HERS Index** (what the final blower-door and duct-leakage tests certify)

The first two are at permit time. The third is at certificate of occupancy. The plan checker is looking for the modeled HERS Index to be at or below the target, with confidence that as-built will land near the modeled value.

## What this guide does not cover

- **Solar PV sizing for net-zero projects** — separate technical analysis with the HERS rater.
- **Heat pump sizing methodology** (Manual J, etc.) — the rater coordinates this with the mechanical designer; not directly part of the HERS calculation.
- **Other certifications** — Passive House, LEED, Phius all have separate certification paths that may overlap with HERS but are not equivalent.
