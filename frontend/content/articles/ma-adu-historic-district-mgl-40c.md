# Historic District Review for Massachusetts ADUs

Most articles on Massachusetts ADU law focus on Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024 and the state-floor preemption framework. There is a second statutory layer that operates entirely separately and can extend permit timelines by 60 to 120 days: historic district review under **MGL Chapter 40C**.

This guide explains why Ch 40C is not preempted by Ch 150, what historical commissions can and cannot regulate, the standard review process, and design choices that survive both reviews efficiently.

## The two-statute framework

Massachusetts ADU permits route through two separate statutory tracks:

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleVII/Chapter40A/Section3 | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: MGL Ch 40A § 3 — by-right protected use after Ch 150 § 8]

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleVII/Chapter40C | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: MGL Ch 40C — Historic Districts Act, separate authority]

Chapter 40A is the zoning-enabling statute. Chapter 150 amended it to make ADUs by-right in single-family zones. Owner-occupancy, special permits for a single ADU, parking above one space — all preempted under the Ch 40A framework.

Chapter 40C is the Historic Districts Act. It authorizes municipalities to designate local historic districts and establish historical commissions to administer them. The commissions review exterior alterations to structures within the district under design-review standards specific to that district's character study.

These are different statutory authorities. Chapter 150 amended Chapter 40A. It did not amend Chapter 40C. Historic district authority survives intact.

## What historical commissions can regulate

Within a designated local historic district, the commission has authority over **exterior alterations visible from a public way**. The standard list:

- New construction (including a new detached ADU)
- Additions and exterior changes to existing structures (including conversion ADUs that change the exterior)
- Demolition or partial demolition
- Materials, fenestration, roof pitch, massing
- Exterior signage, fencing, landscape elements that affect district character
- Setting and view-shed considerations from the public way

The commission's standard of review is whether the proposed work is "appropriate" given the district's character. Most districts have a published character study that describes the architectural style, materials, and detailing typical of the district.

## What historical commissions cannot regulate

The commission's authority is limited to exterior alterations affecting district character. They do not have authority over:

- Interior changes (an interior conversion ADU with no exterior change is generally outside their scope)
- Use of the property (whether the new space is an ADU, a home office, a workshop — the commission's review is about appearance, not function)
- Changes that are not visible from any public way (rare in dense historic districts but possible on a parcel with sufficient setback or fencing)
- Building code, energy code, plumbing, electrical, fire, or other technical compliance — these route through 780 CMR / 248 CMR / 527 CMR and the building department

Because of this scope limit, **interior conversion ADUs typically do not require historical commission review**. A finished basement, a converted attic, or an in-law-suite added entirely within the existing building footprint with no exterior alterations sits outside Ch 40C jurisdiction even when the parcel is in a covered district.

## Typical review process

The standard timeline for Ch 40C review on an ADU:

1. **Pre-application meeting** (optional but strongly recommended) — 30 to 60 minutes with commission staff or a commissioner to walk through proposed work and surface design concerns early.
2. **Application filing** with drawings, materials specifications, and the contractor's design narrative.
3. **Public notice period** — typically 14 days under the local bylaw.
4. **Hearing** at the commission's monthly or bi-monthly meeting. Applicant or representative presents; abutters and members of the public can comment.
5. **Decision** — Certificate of Appropriateness (approved as proposed), Certificate of Hardship (approved with conditions), or Denial.
6. **Filing of certificate** with the building department before the building permit can issue.

Total timeline: 60 to 120 days depending on the commission's meeting schedule and any required revisions. Some commissions meet monthly; some meet every six weeks. Faster timelines require the application to be clean and the design to require minimal revision.

## Cities with multiple historic districts

The five fully-researched cities in PermitMonkey's covered set all have multiple historic districts:

- **Boston** — 11 Landmarks Commission districts plus area-architectural-control districts. The Boston Landmarks Commission administers under both Ch 40C and a separate special-act authority for the city's older designated districts.
- **Cambridge** — Old Cambridge, Mid Cambridge, Avon Hill, Half Crown-Marsh, Harvard Square (CHC oversight), plus newer designations.
- **Somerville** — Old West Somerville, Spring Hill, Westwood Road, plus individual landmarks.
- **Newton** — Newton Centre, Chestnut Hill (partial), Newtonville, Auburndale, West Newton Hill.
- **Brookline** — Cottage Farm, Pill Hill, Graffam-McKay, plus individual landmarks.

For any parcel in any of these cities, verify historic district status against the city's GIS layer before drafting plans. The city's own GIS is authoritative; do not rely on neighbor-by-neighbor knowledge.

## Design choices that survive Ch 40C efficiently

When a parcel is in a covered district, certain design approaches consistently work better than others:

- **Conversion over new construction** — converting basement, attic, garage, or carriage-house space avoids the most aggressive Ch 40C scrutiny. The exterior change may be limited to a new entrance door or window, both of which are reviewable but less contentious than a full new structure.
- **Subordinate massing** — an attached ADU addition that is visibly subordinate to the primary dwelling (lower roof line, smaller footprint, less prominent fenestration) reads as appropriate to most commissions.
- **Materials matching** — using the same siding, roofing, and trim materials as the existing primary, especially when those materials reflect the district's character study.
- **Detailing matching** — roof pitch, eave detail, window proportion, sash style. Period-appropriate detailing helps even on otherwise modern designs.
- **Avoiding street-facing detached construction** — a detached ADU sited at the rear of the parcel, screened by the primary dwelling and existing landscaping, faces less commission scrutiny than one sited near a public way.

## When the commission denies

If the historical commission denies a Certificate of Appropriateness, the practical paths forward are:

- **Revise and re-submit** — most denials come with feedback. Revising the design to address the feedback typically resolves on the next hearing.
- **Apply for a Certificate of Hardship** — Ch 40C contemplates situations where compliance with the design standard would impose unreasonable hardship. The standard is high but not impossible.
- **Appeal to court** under the Massachusetts Administrative Procedure Act — slow and expensive, generally a last resort.
- **Reduce scope to interior-only conversion** — if the original plan included exterior changes but the underlying ADU could be achieved with interior-only conversion, dropping the exterior work removes the parcel from Ch 40C jurisdiction.

## What this guide does not cover

- **National Register vs local historic district** — National Register listing is honorific (federal recognition for tax purposes) and does not impose review requirements at the local level. Local historic districts under Ch 40C are the operative authority. A parcel can be on the National Register without being in a local district, and vice versa.
- **Demolition delay bylaws** — many MA municipalities have demolition delay bylaws that apply to historically significant structures even outside designated districts. These typically delay (90-180 days) rather than prohibit demolition. Different from Ch 40C review.
- **Federal historic preservation tax credits** — Section 47 of the IRC offers tax credits for certified historic rehabilitation. The certification process is separate from local Ch 40C review.
