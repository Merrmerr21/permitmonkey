# Boston ADU Setback Requirements 2026

Boston's zoning framework is structurally different from any other Massachusetts municipality. The Boston Zoning Code is enacted under a special-act statute (the Boston Zoning Enabling Act, originally passed 1956) rather than under the standard MGL Chapter 40A enabling framework that governs the rest of the state. Practically, this means Boston's setback and dimensional rules live in per-neighborhood articles, and Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024 interacts with the Boston code differently than it does with a standard Ch 40A bylaw.

This guide walks through how Boston setbacks actually work for ADU permits, what the BPDA Neighborhood Housing Zoning initiative is changing, and how the state-floor protections apply on top.

## The Boston special-act zoning framework

[source: https://library.municode.com/ma/boston/codes/redevelopment_authority | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: Boston Zoning Code on Municode — special-act framework]

[source: https://www.bostonplans.org/planning-zoning/zoning-initiatives/neighborhood-housing | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: BPDA Neighborhood Housing Zoning — per-neighborhood ADU amendment initiative]

The Boston Zoning Code is structured around city-wide articles (covering process, definitions, and certain across-the-board rules) plus per-neighborhood articles that govern dimensional standards for specific neighborhoods (Article 51 for Brighton, Article 56 for Hyde Park, Article 65 for Allston, etc.).

For ADU work, the relevant article depends on which neighborhood the parcel sits in. Each neighborhood article has its own base zoning subdistricts (residential, commercial, mixed-use) with their own setback, height, and FAR requirements.

The complication: Boston's special-act framework does not automatically map onto Ch 150 of 2024 the way a standard Ch 40A bylaw does. The state law was drafted to amend Ch 40A; the Boston Zoning Code is a separate statute. EOHLC has taken the position that Ch 150's protected-use framework applies to Boston via the spirit and intent of the law, but the formal mechanism is contested in some readings.

The practical answer: Ch 150 applies to Boston for ADU permitting purposes. EOHLC has been clear in its FAQ. The contested-reading discussion is academic for almost every contractor's permit, but worth knowing if a Boston plan checker invokes the framework distinction.

## BPDA Neighborhood Housing Zoning — the rollout

The BPDA (Boston Planning & Development Agency) is amending the per-neighborhood articles one neighborhood at a time to incorporate ADU-enabling language and align with Ch 150. Status as of mid-2026:

- **Mattapan** (Article 60-equivalent) — adopted February 2024, fully ADU-enabled.
- **Roslindale** (Article 67) — draft in progress as of 2026-05-03, not yet adopted.
- **West Roxbury** (Article 68) — draft in progress.
- **Hyde Park** (Article 56) — draft in progress.
- All other neighborhoods — no published timeline; the existing pre-Ch 150 article applies.

For neighborhoods where the article has been amended, the local article governs (subject to state-floor preemption on owner-occupancy / parking ceiling). For neighborhoods where the article has not been amended, the existing article applies plus state-floor preemption.

## Setback rules by neighborhood — the practical landscape

Setbacks vary by base zoning subdistrict within each neighborhood article. The general pattern:

- **Residential subdistricts in dense neighborhoods** (Back Bay, South End, Beacon Hill, Charlestown) — setbacks often 0 feet front and side, with rear setbacks varying. Boston's rowhouse fabric does not typically support detached ADUs; conversion of basement, attic, or rear ell space is the common path.
- **Residential subdistricts in mid-density neighborhoods** (Jamaica Plain, Roxbury, Dorchester, Allston, Brighton) — setbacks typically 5-15 feet front, 5-10 feet side, 15-30 feet rear. Detached ADUs more feasible on larger parcels.
- **Residential subdistricts in lower-density neighborhoods** (West Roxbury, Hyde Park, Roslindale, Readville) — setbacks 15-30 feet front, 5-15 feet side, 25-30 feet rear. Detached ADUs commonly feasible.

Specific dimensional values require reading the controlling neighborhood article on Municode. The Boston Zoning Code is JS-rendered on Municode, which means static fetches return empty page bodies — you have to view it in a browser session.

## The state floor that applies regardless of neighborhood

Three protections apply to every Boston ADU regardless of which neighborhood article governs:

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleVII/Chapter40A/Section3 | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: MGL Ch 40A § 3 — by-right protected use]

[source: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/accessory-dwelling-units | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: 760 CMR 71.00 — EOHLC implementing regulation]

- **Size cap** — lesser of 900 sqft or 50% of primary dwelling's gross floor area.
- **Owner-occupancy** — preempted. Cannot be required.
- **Parking** — capped at one additional space, zero within 0.5 miles of qualifying transit. In Boston, qualifying transit covers most of the city — every Red, Orange, Blue, Green, and Silver Line stop, every commuter rail station, every named MBTA bus station.

The state floor preempts conflicting Boston requirements. It does not preempt the dimensional setbacks themselves — those are FORM, locally controlled.

## Common Boston ADU setback corrections

Plan checkers at Boston Inspectional Services Department (ISD) issue a recurring set of setback-related corrections:

- **"Show setback dimensions on site plan."** Always include dimensions to all four property lines for both the existing primary and the proposed ADU.
- **"Confirm front setback compliance."** Boston's neighborhood articles often define "front" relative to the primary street frontage; corner lots and irregular parcels have nuance.
- **"Show fire-separation distance under 780 CMR R302.1."** When the ADU is within 5 feet of a lot line, the exterior wall requires fire-resistance ratings. Setback compliance and fire-separation compliance are different rules; both apply.
- **"Confirm setback applies in the controlling subdistrict."** The plan checker is verifying which subdistrict the parcel is in; cite the neighborhood article and subdistrict explicitly on the cover sheet.

## What good Boston ADU plan packages include

- Cover sheet protected-use declaration referencing MGL Ch 40A § 3 and 760 CMR 71.00.
- Code summary table with 780 CMR 10th Edition (IRC 2021 base), 225 CMR 22 Appendix RC (Specialized Opt-In Code), 248 CMR (plumbing), 527 CMR 12 (electrical).
- Boston Zoning Code citation by article number and subdistrict for the parcel.
- Setback dimensions to all four property lines for both structures, with the controlling neighborhood-article requirement noted.
- BPDA Neighborhood Housing Zoning status if applicable (Mattapan adopted; others in progress).
- Historic district status under MGL Ch 40C and Boston Landmarks Commission jurisdiction. Boston has 11 Landmarks Commission districts plus area-architectural-control overlays.
- Energy compliance path — Specialized Opt-In Code, HERS rater identified, target rating.
- MBTA proximity declaration if within 0.5 miles. In Boston this almost always applies.
- BFD (Boston Fire Department) plan-review consideration if applicable. BFD reviews plans separately from ISD on certain projects.

## Common Boston-specific gotchas

- **Article 80 thresholds** — Boston's Article 80 (large project review) does not typically apply to a single ADU on a small parcel. Confirm the project size against the article's thresholds (typically 50,000+ sqft of new construction). For a typical ADU, Article 80 review is not required.
- **Rowhouse subdivision limits** — in dense residential subdistricts, the existing rowhouse fabric may not support a detached ADU. Conversion of internal space or rear-ell expansion is the realistic approach.
- **BPDA design review** in some subdistricts — separate from zoning, BPDA's design review applies in certain mixed-use and waterfront subdistricts. Most residential ADUs sit outside BPDA design review jurisdiction.
- **Inclusionary Development Policy (IDP)** — does not typically apply to a single ADU, but verify against the policy's project-size thresholds for any project that includes additional dwelling units beyond the single ADU.
