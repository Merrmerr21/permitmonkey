# ADU Setbacks in Massachusetts — State Law vs. Local Bylaw

The single most-asked question we get from contractors after Chapter 150 of 2024 is: "If state law made ADUs by-right, can the city still impose setbacks?" The answer is yes — setbacks are FORM, not USE, and FORM remains locally controlled. This guide explains where the line is, where typical Massachusetts cities draw their setbacks, and the narrow cases where a setback rule might still be preempted.

## The USE / FORM line

The state preemption framework under Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024 prohibits municipalities from regulating the **use** of ADUs (whether you can build one, owner-occupancy, special permits, parking quantity near MBTA service). It does **not** prohibit municipalities from regulating the **form** the building takes — setbacks, height, lot coverage, FAR, bulk, mass.

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/SessionLaws/Acts/2024/Chapter150 | retrieved: 2026-05-05 | citation: St. 2024, c. 150 §§ 7-8 — preemption scope is USE; FORM remains local]

Where the line is drawn matters because setbacks can effectively constrain whether a project is buildable. A 25-foot rear setback on a 50-foot-deep lot leaves you very little room. The state law guarantees the legal right to build; it doesn't guarantee the dimensional fit.

## How to read your local bylaw for setbacks

Setbacks live in the dimensional-standards table of the local zoning ordinance, almost always indexed by zone designation:

1. **Identify the zone.** Pull the address up on the city's GIS / property viewer. Note the zoning district (e.g., "Boston 1F-7000," "Cambridge Residence A-2," "Newton SR-3").

2. **Find the dimensional table.** It's usually in the chapter or article on residential zones. Look for column headings like "Front Yard," "Side Yard," "Rear Yard."

3. **Check for ADU-specific footnotes.** Some cities have added ADU-specific dimensional rules (often more permissive than the underlying zone) under the Neighborhood Housing Zoning amendments. If those exist, they govern.

4. **Check for corner-lot adjustments.** Corner lots typically have two front yards and tighter side yards on the street side. Read the corner-lot footnotes carefully.

5. **Check for accessory-structure setbacks.** Detached ADUs may follow accessory-structure setbacks (often more lenient on the rear yard) rather than principal-building setbacks.

## Setback rules that might still be preempted

While setbacks are generally local, there are narrow cases where a specific setback requirement might still be preempted under state law:

### Setback requirements that effectively prohibit ADUs

If a city's setback rules combined with its FAR / lot-coverage rules effectively make ADUs un-buildable on the average single-family lot in a residential zone, the rules may be challengeable as a constructive prohibition. State law preempts USE; a setback regime that makes USE impossible runs into the spirit of the preemption.

This is a litigation-heavy theory. We've seen it referenced in EOHLC technical assistance memos but not yet tested in Massachusetts courts as of May 2026.

[source: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/eohlc-affordable-homes-act-adu-resources | retrieved: 2026-05-05 | citation: EOHLC TA on constructive-prohibition setback regimes]

### Different setbacks for ADUs vs. principal buildings

Some cities have set differential setbacks — stricter for ADUs than for the underlying principal building. Differential rules that single out ADUs for less-favorable treatment are vulnerable to a preemption challenge. The legal theory: state law makes ADUs by-right, so the city cannot impose a use-discriminatory dimensional restriction on the use it has been required to allow.

This is a stronger theory than constructive prohibition but still untested in MA courts.

### Owner-occupancy-conditioned setbacks

If a city's setback rules apply only when the property is owner-occupied (or only when it's not), the conditioning is preempted. Owner-occupancy can't be a permit condition; therefore it can't be a dimensional-standard condition either.

## What you should do, practically

For most contractors building most ADUs in most MA cities, the setbacks in the local zoning ordinance govern. Don't try to dispute standard setback dimensions; comply with them.

Where it pays to verify the preemption theory:

- **Setback combinations that constructively prohibit.** If your specific lot can't fit any ADU under the dimensional rules, you may have a constructive-prohibition claim. Don't draft this argument yourself — engage a Massachusetts zoning attorney.
- **ADU-specific differential setbacks.** Worth a polite preemption letter; many cities will withdraw rather than litigate.
- **Owner-occupancy-conditioned dimensional rules.** Always cite the state preemption.

For everything else: design within the dimensional standards.

## Setbacks vs. parking — different preemption layers

Don't confuse the setback discussion with the parking preemption. Parking quantity is preempted by Chapter 150 (one space max, zero near MBTA). Parking dimensions (size of a parking space, drive aisle width) are FORM, locally controlled.

| Item | USE/FORM | Preempted? |
|---|---|---|
| Parking quantity | USE | Yes (≤1 space, 0 near MBTA) |
| Parking dimensions | FORM | No |
| Driveway width | FORM | No |
| Front setback | FORM | No |
| Side setback | FORM | No |
| Rear setback | FORM | No |
| Building height | FORM | No |
| Lot coverage | FORM | No |
| FAR | FORM | No |
| Owner-occupancy | USE | Yes (cannot be required) |
| Special-permit hearing | USE | Yes (cannot be required) |
| Family-only occupancy | USE | Yes (cannot be required) |

## What this means for your project

When you're scoping an ADU in Massachusetts:

1. Get the eligibility verdict on the address — confirms whether ADUs are by-right (state law floor).
2. Pull the zoning district from the city's GIS.
3. Read the dimensional standards for that district. Comply with them in your design.
4. If the dimensional standards make the ADU un-buildable on the lot, document the constraint and consult a zoning attorney before assuming a preemption claim.
5. Submit. If the corrections letter cites a setback, FAR, or height issue: comply or revise. If it cites owner-occupancy, special permit, or excessive parking: cite the state preemption.
