# Owner-Occupancy Is Preempted in Massachusetts

If you are applying for an ADU permit anywhere in Massachusetts and the city's correction letter includes "owner shall reside in either the primary dwelling or the ADU" — that requirement is preempted by state law. Cities cannot require owner-occupancy of either unit on a Protected Use ADU parcel, period.

This guide explains what Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024 actually changed, what the related family-relationship preemption is and why both matter, and the exact citation pattern that resolves most owner-occupancy corrections.

## The statutory mechanism

Two amendments did the work.

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/SessionLaws/Acts/2024/Chapter150 | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: St. 2024, c. 150 §§ 7-8 — Affordable Homes Act ADU provisions]

Section 7 amended MGL Ch 40A § 1A to define an "accessory dwelling unit" as a self-contained dwelling unit. The definition is silent on who has to live in it — and that silence matters. The pre-2025 ADU definition in many local bylaws assumed a family member or owner-occupant. The state-level definition does not.

Section 8 amended MGL Ch 40A § 3 to make ADUs a by-right protected use in single-family-zoned districts. The protected-use status carries with it the implication that conditions cities historically attached to ADU permits — owner-occupancy, family-only occupancy, special-permit hearings, additional parking — cannot be imposed in conflict with the state floor.

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleVII/Chapter40A/Section3 | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: MGL Ch 40A § 3 — current text after Ch 150 § 8 protected use paragraph]

EOHLC's implementing regulation closes the loop with explicit language on what cities can and cannot do.

[source: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/accessory-dwelling-units | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: 760 CMR 71.00 — owner-occupancy preemption]

## What "preempted" means in practice

Three consequences for any Massachusetts ADU permit:

1. **The city cannot require owner-occupancy** of either dwelling on the parcel as a condition of approval. Whether the owner lives in the primary, in the ADU, or in neither, the city has no authority to deny on that basis.

2. **The city cannot require family relationship** between the occupant of the ADU and the owner of the parcel. Pre-2025 "in-law apartment" ordinances that limited ADU occupancy to relatives are preempted.

3. **The city cannot impose conditions that effectively require owner-occupancy** — for example, requiring two parking spaces unless owner-occupied, or requiring a deed restriction that limits transfer-on-sale to owner-occupant buyers. These are downstream consequences of the same preemption.

## What the city CAN still regulate

To draw the line clearly:

- **Short-term rental** (Airbnb-style) — the city can restrict or prohibit short-term rental of the ADU under separate authority. Many MA cities do.
- **Lot occupancy** — the city can enforce dwelling-unit count limits per lot. Two ADUs (one primary plus two accessory) typically requires a special permit even after Chapter 150.
- **Deed restrictions for separate matters** — the city can impose restrictions tied to inclusionary zoning, affordability set-asides, or other state-authorized programs.

What the city cannot do is require owner-occupancy of either dwelling as a baseline condition of the by-right ADU permit.

## When a Massachusetts city's correction letter requires owner-occupancy

Use this response. The structure is "cite, apply, request."

> "The proposed ADU is a Protected Use under MGL Ch 40A § 3 (as amended by St. 2024, c. 150, § 8) and is implemented by 760 CMR 71.00. State law preempts owner-occupancy requirements on either dwelling for a Protected Use ADU.
>
> Correction item [#] requires that '[exact city language]'. This requirement is preempted by state law.
>
> We respectfully request the comment be withdrawn, or revised to remove the owner-occupancy condition. The remaining conditions in correction item [#] are accepted as written."

Most plan checkers acknowledge and revise after one round. EOHLC has issued public guidance on the preemption; the position is settled.

## The "in-law apartment" naming trap

Some Massachusetts municipalities historically permitted only "in-law apartments" — a term of art meaning an ADU restricted to a family member of the primary-dwelling owner. Pre-2025, this was a legitimate (if restrictive) local approach.

After Chapter 150, the family-relationship restriction is preempted. But the **name** survives in some local bylaws. If your city's bylaw refers to "in-law apartments" and tries to apply pre-2025 family-only restrictions, the bylaw is partially preempted.

The preemption is selective. The bylaw can still:

- Name the use (the label is not preempted).
- Set dimensional standards (form is locally controlled).
- Apply historic district overlays under MGL Ch 40C separately.

The bylaw cannot:

- Restrict occupancy to family members of the owner.
- Require owner-occupancy of either dwelling.
- Treat the ADU differently for occupancy purposes than the state's protected-use framework does.

When responding to corrections that cite an "in-law apartment" provision, identify the specific subsection that applies the family-only restriction, cite the preemption, and request the comment be revised against the state floor.

## What this guide does not cover

- **Building-code occupancy classifications.** 780 CMR 10th Edition (the MA State Building Code) has its own occupancy provisions that govern construction. Those are not preempted by Chapter 150 — they apply to physical construction, not to who can live there.
- **Federal fair-housing law.** State preemption operates under MA law. Federal Fair Housing Act protections apply on top, separately.
- **Existing deed restrictions.** If the parcel already has a private deed restriction (from an HOA or a prior owner-imposed covenant), that is a private-law matter outside the city's permit authority. Resolve those separately with counsel.
