# Cambridge ADU 2026 Contractor Guide

Cambridge is one of the cities where Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024 changed the math. Before the act, Cambridge required a special permit for accessory dwelling units in most residential districts. After February 2, 2025, an ADU on a single-family parcel is by-right under state law — Cambridge cannot require a special permit for a single ADU.

What Cambridge can still regulate is FORM: dimensional standards, lot coverage, FAR, design standards, historic district review. This guide walks through what survived state preemption and what to do when a Cambridge plan checker invokes a preempted rule.

## The state floor that applies to every Cambridge parcel

Three citations govern any Cambridge ADU permit. Memorize them — they appear on almost every defensible response letter.

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleVII/Chapter40A/Section3 | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: MGL Ch 40A § 3 — by-right ADU paragraph after Ch 150 § 8]

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/SessionLaws/Acts/2024/Chapter150 | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: St. 2024, c. 150 §§ 7-8 — Affordable Homes Act ADU provisions]

[source: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/accessory-dwelling-units | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: 760 CMR 71.00 — EOHLC implementing regulation, effective 2025-02-02]

The state floor caps the ADU at the lesser of 900 square feet or 50 percent of the primary dwelling's gross floor area. It blocks owner-occupancy requirements on either dwelling. It limits parking to one additional space, and to zero additional spaces within 0.5 miles of qualifying transit (which in Cambridge means almost the entire city — every Red Line stop, the green-line extensions, key bus station designations).

## Where Cambridge form-controls bind tighter than state floor

Cambridge's residential districts are R-1, R-1A, R-1B, R-2, R-3, A-1, A-2, B, C, C-1, C-1A, and several mixed-use districts that allow residential. The dimensional standards that bind hardest on small parcels:

- **Lot coverage** — Cambridge enforces lot coverage caps that frequently bind before setbacks do. A typical Residence A or B parcel allows 25-35% lot coverage. With an existing primary dwelling at 30% coverage, an ADU footprint of even 600 square feet can push the parcel over.
- **FAR** — Floor area ratio caps run 0.5-1.0 in most residential districts. Combined primary + ADU FAR has to fit under the cap.
- **Setbacks** — 10 to 25 feet front, 7.5 to 10 feet side, 25 to 30 feet rear in most districts. Detached ADU placement gets squeezed quickly on a 5,000-square-foot lot.

The state floor does **not** preempt these dimensional rules. It preempts the by-right protected use; the form remains Cambridge's call within reasonableness limits.

[source: https://www.cambridgema.gov/CDD/zoninganddevelopment/zoningordinance | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: Cambridge Zoning Ordinance — Residence A/B/C dimensional standards]

## The Specialized Opt-In Energy Code

Cambridge has adopted the Specialized Opt-In Code under 225 CMR 22 Appendix RC. Every new ADU must comply with the higher-performance envelope and pass HERS rating.

[source: https://www.mass.gov/info-details/specialized-stretch-code | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: 225 CMR 22 Appendix RC — Specialized Opt-In Energy Code adopted by Cambridge]

What this means in practice for a contractor:

- **All-electric or near-zero fossil fuel** for new construction. A heat pump for space conditioning. Heat-pump water heater or equivalent. Resistance backup is permitted but increases the HERS calculation.
- **Envelope** — wall R-values around R-21 to R-30 plus continuous insulation, ceiling around R-49 minimum, foundation R-15 minimum, U-factor 0.30 windows or better.
- **HERS rating** — typically 45-55 target depending on size and orientation. The HERS rater is a separate scope; contractors should engage one at design phase, not at certificate of occupancy.

Plan reviewers at Cambridge Inspectional Services Department flag missing HERS documentation as a routine correction. Show the energy compliance path on the cover sheet to avoid the back-and-forth.

## Historical Commission overlays you cannot ignore

Cambridge has multiple local historic districts that trigger Cambridge Historical Commission review independent of zoning. The big ones:

- Old Cambridge
- Mid Cambridge
- Avon Hill
- Half Crown-Marsh
- Harvard Square (CHC oversight, but the BPDA-equivalent process applies)

If your parcel sits in one of these, you face a 60-120 day Historical Commission process layered on top of the zoning approval. The Commission's authority comes from MGL Ch 40C — a different statute than Ch 40A — and is **not** preempted by the state ADU law.

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/GeneralLaws/PartI/TitleVII/Chapter40C | retrieved: 2026-05-07 | citation: MGL Ch 40C — Historic Districts Act, separate authority not preempted by Ch 150]

What matters: the CHC reviews exterior alterations only. An interior conversion ADU sits outside their jurisdiction. A detached new-construction ADU visible from a public way is squarely within it. Plan accordingly — a basement conversion in Old Cambridge is a 4-week zoning approval; a detached ADU in the same district is a 16-week project.

## When Cambridge requires owner-occupancy or extra parking

Both are preempted. The response template:

> "The proposed ADU is a Protected Use under MGL Ch 40A § 3 (as amended by St. 2024, c. 150, § 8) and is implemented by 760 CMR 71.00. The owner-occupancy [or parking-additional-space] requirement in [Cambridge bylaw section] is preempted by state law to the extent it conflicts with the protected-use framework. We respectfully request the comment be withdrawn or revised to remove the preempted condition."

If the plan checker pushes back, escalate. EOHLC has been clear in its FAQ that owner-occupancy preemption is statewide and absolute. The Cambridge Inspectional Services Department has updated its internal guidance — most reviewers have caught up. If you draw a reviewer who has not, ask for the citation they are relying on. They will not have one.

## What good Cambridge ADU plan packages include up front

To minimize the corrections cycle:

- **Cover sheet declaration**: "This accessory dwelling unit is a protected use under MGL Ch 40A § 3 as amended."
- **Code summary table**: 780 CMR 10th Edition (IRC 2021 base with MA amendments), 225 CMR 22 Appendix RC, 248 CMR 10 (plumbing), 527 CMR 12 (electrical), MGL Ch 148 §§ 26F / 26F½ (alarms).
- **HERS rater identified by name** with the planned compliance path.
- **Lot coverage and FAR calculation** showing primary + ADU under district maximums.
- **Setback dimensions** to all property lines, with a clean tie to the controlling district standards.
- **Historic district status** on the cover sheet — yes, no, or "verified not within an established district" with the parcel ID.

A clean package gets through Cambridge ISD in 4-6 weeks. A package that fights state preemption while missing energy compliance documentation gets to 12-16 weeks fast.

## What this guide does not cover

- Specific dimensional values per district. Cambridge's bylaw has nuance (R-1A allows different setbacks than R-1; the C district has its own table). Look up the specific district code for your parcel and confirm against the published table.
- Wetlands and floodplain. If you are within 100 feet of a regulated wetland or in a FEMA zone, MGL Ch 131 § 40 and the Cambridge Conservation Commission process apply on top of everything else.
- Construction-phase permits (sub-permits for plumbing, gas, electrical). Each has its own filing process at ISD; this guide is scoped to the building permit.
