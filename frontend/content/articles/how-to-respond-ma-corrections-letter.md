# How to Respond to a Massachusetts Plan Check Corrections Letter

A corrections letter is not a rejection. It's a structured list of items the plan checker wants resolved before issuing the permit. Most Massachusetts ADU corrections letters have 5-15 items; a typical project clears them in 1-2 cycles. Here's the playbook for getting through them efficiently — without expanding scope and without stepping on a state-law preemption that could resolve the item in one paragraph.

## Step 1 — Categorize each item into one of four buckets

Read the letter front-to-back once before you start drafting anything. As you read, sort each item into one of four buckets:

### Bucket A: State-preempted (USE)

The item is asking for something Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024 prohibits. Examples: owner-occupancy condition, special-permit hearing, parking near MBTA, family-only occupancy.

[source: https://malegislature.gov/Laws/SessionLaws/Acts/2024/Chapter150 | retrieved: 2026-05-05 | citation: St. 2024, c. 150 §§ 7-8 — preempted USE regulation]

**Strategy:** Cite the state law and ask the city to withdraw the item. Don't comply. Don't argue. Cite, then propose withdrawal.

### Bucket B: Locally controlled (FORM) — comply

The item is asking for something the local code legitimately controls. Examples: setback dimensions, building height, lot coverage, FAR, drainage details, fire apparatus access.

**Strategy:** Comply with the request. Revise the drawing or add the missing dimension; resubmit.

### Bucket C: Independent regime — comply

The item is from a regulatory layer that runs in parallel to zoning: 780 CMR (state building code), 225 CMR 22 (energy code), 521 CMR (accessibility), Wetlands Protection Act, Boston Landmarks Commission, etc.

**Strategy:** Comply with the request. The state-law preemption framework only applies to zoning USE; these other regimes have their own preemption layers and almost always govern.

### Bucket D: Documentation gap — clarify

The item is asking for something you've already provided, or for something that doesn't apply to the project. Examples: "Provide foundation drawings" when foundation is already shown on S-101; "Provide accessibility analysis" when the project is owner-occupied single-family.

**Strategy:** Provide the verbatim sheet and detail reference, or explain the inapplicability. Don't redraw what you already have; just point at it.

## Step 2 — Draft Bucket A responses first

Bucket A items get resolved with citations, not redesigns. They're the cheapest items to clear and the highest-value cleanup if the plan checker accepts the citation.

The four-element preemption response template:

1. **Quote the correction verbatim.**
2. **State the preemption.** "This requirement is preempted by Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024, §X."
3. **Provide the citation chain.** MGL Ch 40A § 3, as amended by St. 2024, c. 150, §7. Include 760 CMR 71.00 if the implementing regulation has the specific clause.
4. **Propose the next step.** "Please confirm that this item is withdrawn so we can finalize the submittal."

Example for a preempted owner-occupancy item:

> **City correction:** "ADU may only be permitted if the owner occupies one of the dwelling units."
>
> **Response:** Owner-occupancy requirements were prohibited by Chapter 150 of the Acts of 2024, §7, which amended MGL Ch 40A §3 to make ADUs by-right without conditions on occupancy. The implementing regulation at 760 CMR 71.00 §4 confirms that occupancy conditions are not permissible. Please confirm that this item is withdrawn so we can finalize the submittal.

## Step 3 — Draft Bucket B and Bucket C responses

These are compliance items. Write the response as concise factual update on the drawing or the documentation:

> **City correction:** "Side setback shown as 4 feet; minimum is 5 feet per zone Residence-A dimensional standards."
>
> **Response:** Plans revised. Updated side setback is 5 feet 6 inches. See revised Sheet A-101, dimension callout at grid line B/2.

Notice what's NOT in this response:
- No apology
- No explanation of why the original was wrong
- No commitment beyond the specific correction asked
- No expansion of scope to "while we're at it, we also want to..."

Stay tight. The plan checker is reading 50-100 pages of corrections responses across multiple projects in a session. They want to find the dimension on the revised drawing and check it off.

## Step 4 — Bucket D documentation gaps

These are the trickiest because they require you to push back politely:

> **City correction:** "Provide accessibility analysis under 521 CMR."
>
> **Response:** This project is an owner-occupied single-family residence with a non-public ADU. Per 521 CMR 1.07, single-family residences are exempt from MAAB scoping requirements until the work cost exceeds 30% of the full and fair cash value of the existing structure. The project work cost is $X, well below the $Y threshold (30% of the $Z FCV per the 2026 city assessment). Accessibility analysis is therefore not within the scope of this submittal.

The Bucket D response cites the rule that exempts you, names the specific numbers, and concludes with "not within scope." It's polite, factual, and complete.

## What NOT to do

The four most-common contractor mistakes on corrections-letter responses:

### 1. Don't argue Bucket A items, cite them

A long argument about why the city is wrong reads as adversarial. A short citation chain reads as a routine compliance update. State preemption is an objective legal fact; treat it like one.

### 2. Don't expand scope on Bucket B items

If the correction asks to fix a side setback, fix the side setback. Don't redraw the floor plan to "while we're at it..." All scope expansions extend review by a cycle and risk introducing new corrections.

### 3. Don't ignore Bucket C items hoping they go away

Energy code, accessibility, fire — these regimes have their own enforcement mechanisms. The plan checker has the authority to enforce them and will. Comply on the first cycle.

### 4. Don't blanket-deny Bucket D items

Saying "this doesn't apply" without citing the rule that exempts you reads as evasive. Cite the exemption rule, name the numbers, conclude with the scope statement.

## Format and delivery

A clean Massachusetts corrections-response package has:

- **Cover memo** (1-2 pages): identifies the project, the corrections letter date, the response date, and a summary list of items addressed.
- **Per-item response** (1 paragraph each): quotes the correction, states the response category (typically: "Plans revised" / "Preemption cited" / "Out of scope"), provides the substantive response.
- **Revised drawings** (only the sheets that changed): with revision clouds and a revision block in the title block.
- **Citations index** (optional but professional): a list of every state law, CMR, and city bylaw citation used in the responses, with URLs.
- **Signed cover sheet** from the registered design professional of record.

Most cities accept PDF submission via the same portal the original submittal went through. Some require physical delivery; check the city's plan-review FAQ.

## Realistic timeline

For a typical Massachusetts ADU corrections letter (8-12 items, mix of all four buckets):

- **Day 1**: Categorize all items. Identify Bucket A items (typically 1-3 in a Massachusetts ADU letter post-Ch 150).
- **Day 2-3**: Draft Bucket A responses. These are fast — citation work, no redrawing.
- **Day 4-7**: Draft Bucket B and C responses, revise drawings.
- **Day 8**: Draft Bucket D responses with exemption citations.
- **Day 9**: Compile package, get RDP signature, submit.

Total: 9 working days for a clean cycle. A second cycle (if needed) is typically 3-5 days because the items are narrower.
