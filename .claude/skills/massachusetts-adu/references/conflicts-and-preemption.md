# Conflicts and Preemption — When State Law Wins

## The Preemption Hierarchy

```
Massachusetts Constitution
    ↓
MGL Ch 40A §§1A, 3 (as amended by St. 2024, c. 150, §§7-8)
    ↓
760 CMR 71.00 (EOHLC implementing regulation)
    ↓
Other statewide codes (780 CMR building, 527 CMR fire, 521 CMR accessibility)
    ↓
Local zoning bylaws, ordinances, general bylaws
```

When a local provision conflicts with the state statute or regulation, the local provision is unenforceable to the extent of the conflict.

## Clear Preemption (City Cannot Win These)

### Owner-Occupancy Requirement
- **State rule:** MGL Ch 40A §3 explicitly prohibits owner-occupancy requirements.
- **Typical city correction:** "Owner must reside in the primary dwelling and attest annually."
- **Response:** This requirement is preempted. Cite MGL Ch 40A §3 as amended by St. 2024, c. 150, §8. No action required beyond noting preemption.

### Special Permit Requirement for a Single ADU
- **State rule:** Single ADU in single-family zone is by-right. No special permit allowed.
- **Typical city correction:** "Applicant must obtain special permit from the ZBA."
- **Response:** This requirement is preempted. Administrative review (site plan review, building permit) only.

### Prohibition on ADUs in Single-Family Zones
- **State rule:** Cities cannot prohibit the use.
- **Typical city correction:** "ADUs are not permitted in [Zone X]" where X is a single-family district.
- **Response:** The prohibition is preempted. The ADU may proceed as of right subject to reasonable regulations.

### Parking Requirement Exceeding State Ceiling
- **State rule:** Max 1 additional space; 0 if within 0.5 mi of transit.
- **Typical city correction:** "Provide 2 parking spaces for the ADU."
- **Response:** Parking requirement exceeds the statutory maximum. 1 space (or 0 if within transit) is the limit.

### Requirement That Renter Be a Family Member
- **State rule:** No family-relationship restriction under state law.
- **Typical city correction:** "ADU may only be rented to a family member of the primary dwelling occupant."
- **Response:** Preempted. The use includes rental without family-relationship conditions (except STR, which is separately regulable).

## Gray Area — Potentially Unreasonable

The statute's "shall not unreasonably restrict" language creates space for reasonableness challenges. These are case-by-case:

### Very Restrictive Setbacks
- Setbacks that effectively make no ADU buildable on typical lots in the zone
- Response approach: comply if possible; if no compliance is possible, flag as unreasonable restriction

### Burdensome Design Review
- Design review processes that take months, impose discretionary standards, or effectively operate as a special permit
- Response approach: comply with administrative portions; push back if discretionary elements appear

### Excessive Fees
- Fees that exceed reasonable cost recovery
- Response approach: pay and flag; fee disputes are rarely worth litigating

### Short-Term Rental Conditions Applied to Long-Term Use
- Cities attempting to apply STR restrictions to an ADU that will be used for LTR
- Response approach: distinguish LTR from STR in the application; cite preemption if city conflates them

## Not Preempted (Cities Retain Authority)

### Dimensional Setbacks (Reasonable)
- Front, side, rear, accessory structure setbacks
- Response approach: comply with the bylaw

### Height and Bulk Limits
- Within reason, cities set these

### Lot Coverage / FAR
- Cities may cap total lot coverage and floor area

### Building Code Compliance (780 CMR)
- Not a zoning issue; state code applies regardless

### Historic District Review (MGL Ch 40C)
- Separate authority; not preempted by Ch 150

### Wetlands Protection (MGL Ch 131 §40)
- Separate authority; not preempted by Ch 150

### Short-Term Rental Regulation
- Cities may regulate or prohibit STR use of the ADU

### Site Plan Review (Administrative)
- Cities may require, but must be non-discretionary

### Permit Fees (Reasonable)
- Cities may charge standard permit fees

### Inspection and Approval Process
- Standard building inspections apply

## How to Respond When a City Correction Conflicts With State Law

1. **Verify the conflict.** Reread the state statute and regulation carefully. Don't claim preemption lightly.
2. **Cite specifically.** "MGL Ch 40A §3 as amended by St. 2024, c. 150, §8, paragraph [X], provides that [exact quote]."
3. **Be polite, not adversarial.** The plan checker is a human; the bylaw may predate Ch 150; the correction may be honest.
4. **Offer a compliance alternative if available.** If there's any path to compliance with both the state floor and local rule, prefer it.
5. **Document everything.** The response letter, the statute citation, and the city's correction should all be in writing.

## Escalation Path

If the city rejects the preemption argument:
1. Request a written explanation of the denial
2. Appeal to the ZBA (Zoning Board of Appeals) — the appeal itself preserves the preemption argument
3. Optional: appeal to Massachusetts Housing Appeals Committee (HAC) or Land Court

PermitMonkey does not handle escalations directly — the contractor and their attorney do. But the response letter should be drafted such that it preserves the arguments for escalation.

## Documentation Standard

Every response letter that invokes preemption should include:
- The exact state statute citation (with amendment reference)
- The exact state regulation citation (760 CMR 71.00)
- A URL to the canonical source
- The specific local correction being challenged
- The specific preemption reasoning

A bare assertion of preemption without citations will not hold up. Do it right.
